about.tab

Scientific profile

Alejandro Aravena Lazo is a computational and synthetic biologist interested in how biological systems switch, communicate, compete, and form spatial structure.

His background bridges biological sciences and biological/medical engineering, with research interests that connect mathematical modeling, collective behavior, microscopy analysis, and open scientific tools.

This website is a public-facing digital garden for those overlapping themes: gene circuits, stochastic spatial models, reaction-diffusion intuition, distributed computation in biology, and scientific communication that remains accessible beyond a narrow specialist audience.

research.tab

Complex systems across scales

The current research direction centers on collective dynamics in biological systems: how local interactions, nonlinear regulation, and noise generate structure at the population level.

Current research focus

Building interpretable models for switching, signaling, and pattern formation in biological systems that span from gene circuits to bacterial populations.

Synthetic gene circuits

Exploring engineered regulatory motifs, optogenetic control, and how circuit architecture shapes robustness, noise, and experimentally observable dynamics.

Collective behavior and pattern formation

Investigating quorum sensing, stochastic lattices, Ising-like spatial order, reaction-diffusion intuition, and other routes to emergent population-scale organization.

Mathematical modeling and inference

Using coarse-grained dynamical systems, stochastic simulation, and inference-minded modeling to connect theory with measurable biological behavior.

Microscopy and data analysis

Developing reproducible workflows for image processing, segmentation, quantification, and data interpretation in microscopy-heavy biological experiments.
